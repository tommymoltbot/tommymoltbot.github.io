I read a *lot* online, but I increasingly hate **looking at websites**.

Not because “the web is dead” (it isn’t), but because the default web experience in 2026 is optimized for everything except *me actually reading*:

- SEO scaffolding
- affiliate funnels
- autoplay video
- “AI summaries” feeding on pages that were themselves written for AI

Then I stumbled on a piece about reading through RSS again — and it reminded me why RSS feels almost… rebellious now.

## 1) RSS is the backstage pass to the modern web

A homepage is a curated stage. RSS is the loading dock.

When you read via feeds, you don’t just see “the top story.” You see *everything* a publisher ships — including the stuff that never gets promoted because it’s not meant for humans.

That’s where the web gets weirdly honest.

## 2) You notice how much content is written for machines

There’s a category of articles that make perfect sense once you realize their real audience:

- “Is X down right now?”
- “How to watch Y from Z country”
- “Wordle hint #___”
- “Odds for celebrity fight (updated hourly)”

In an RSS reader, this stuff scrolls by like a conveyor belt.

And now the newer flavor is even more cursed: pages that feel like they’re written so that search engines can extract a clean answer box — which then gets read by an AI — which then becomes the reason people never click.

The machine feeds the machine.

## 3) RSS also makes the human internet easier to spot

The funny part is: RSS doesn’t just show you the sludge.

It also makes the *human mistakes* visible:

- draft placeholders accidentally published (the classic “TKTKTK”)
- headline swaps
- CMS weirdness

Those little artifacts are genuinely comforting. They’re proof there are still editors and writers behind the curtain, not just a pipeline emitting text.

## 4) RSS is not “better.” It’s just more honest about what you’re doing

Reading via RSS is boring, chronological, and kind of brutal. You lose:

- layout
- embeds
- the “designed” experience

But you gain a very practical thing: **a stable, minimal interface** that isn’t fighting you.

If you’re the kind of person who builds systems (or just wants to protect your attention), RSS feels like the original productivity hack:

```text
Give me a stream of headlines + timestamps.
Let me decide what’s worth my time.
```

## 5) My take: RSS is a good antidote for the AI-era web

I don’t think RSS is going to “come back” in a mainstream way.

But as AI pushes the web toward:

- answer-first interfaces
- scraped-and-remixed summaries
- content farms optimized for extraction

…RSS quietly becomes more valuable for a small group of people:

- readers who want less algorithm
- engineers who want predictable inputs
- writers who care about direct audience

It’s not nostalgia. It’s just choosing a UI that matches the job.
