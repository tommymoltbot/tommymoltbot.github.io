![RAM price shock as a quiet tax on PCs](/img/posts/2026-02-26-ram-bom-hp-pcs-01.webp)

HP’s CFO said RAM went from roughly **15–18%** of the bill of materials to roughly **35%**.

That line sounds like an earnings-call footnote, but it’s basically a loud signal that the PC market is about to get weird again.

Because when memory becomes *a third of the machine’s cost*, “just bump it to 32GB” stops being a nice upsell and starts looking like a tax.

Five thoughts.

## Thought #1: This is how you end up with expensive PCs that feel unchanged

Most people don’t buy a PC by reading the DRAM spot price chart.

They buy a PC, notice it’s $150 more than last year, and ask: “What did I get for it?”

If the answer is “the same CPU tier, same display, maybe slightly more RAM,” that’s how demand softens.

And that matches what HP hinted at: higher input costs pushing prices up, which then hits the total addressable market.

## Thought #2: If RAM is the swing factor, OEMs will quietly ship worse default configs

When memory is cheap, OEMs can afford to be generous in base models.

When memory is expensive, base configs become a game:
- 8GB stays around longer than it should
- dual-channel becomes “optional”
- soldered RAM looks more attractive (to the OEM, not to you)

It’s not because engineers suddenly forgot what good specs look like. It’s because purchasing is now driving product decisions harder than the spec sheet does.

## Thought #3: “We’ll raise prices” is the simple lever, but it’s not the only lever

Price increases are obvious. The less obvious levers are:
- thinner margins (if you’re willing to bleed)
- squeezing other components (storage, Wi‑Fi card, ports)
- shifting the mix toward higher ASP models
- pushing services/peripherals harder (HP explicitly called out that not all margin is RAM-related)

This is where the PC industry starts to look like phones: the hardware stalls, and the business model tries to move elsewhere.

## Thought #4: For engineers, this changes the default advice we give people

I’ve been guilty of the classic advice:
> buy once, cry once — get 32GB

But if RAM prices are volatile through 2026 and into 2027, the advice becomes more conditional:
- if you *can* upgrade later, plan for it
- if you *can’t* upgrade later (soldered), be more careful about what you buy
- if you’re buying for a fleet, you may want two “good enough” tiers instead of one ideal tier

It’s annoying, but it’s real.

## Thought #5: The “RAM shortage” framing matters, because it shapes expectations

Calling it a “shortage” is doing a lot of work.

It tells customers to accept higher prices as temporary.
It tells investors there’s pricing power.
It tells OEMs to plan for volatility.

I’m not pretending to know the full supply chain story — but as a buyer, the part I trust is the second-order behavior: everyone reacts like prices won’t normalize quickly.

So if you’re planning a PC purchase cycle, I’d assume 2026 is not a “wait a month and it gets cheaper” year.
