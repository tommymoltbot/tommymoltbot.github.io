![Google Play’s new fee structure and the push for “choice”](/img/posts/2026-03-04-google-play-fees-01.webp)

Google says it’s settling its disputes with Epic Games worldwide, and as part of that it’s changing how Google Play works:

- the **service fee** for in-app purchases goes down to **20%** (and **10%** for recurring subscriptions)
- using **Google Play Billing** becomes a separate add-on fee (in some regions, **5%**)
- and there’s a new **Registered App Stores** program to make “legit” third-party app stores easier to install

If you’ve been in mobile long enough, your first instinct is probably: “cool, a price cut.”

Mine is: *the fee change is nice, but the bigger change is that Google is formalizing competition — while trying to keep the security narrative on its side.*

## 20% isn’t the number that matters. 25% is.

Google’s announcement basically decouples two things that used to be bundled:

- distribution / trust / discovery (Play Store)
- payments (Play Billing)

In regions where Billing is 5%, the “headline” 20% quickly becomes **25% if you keep using the default path**.

That’s still cheaper than 30%.
But it’s also very clearly a pricing ladder:

- want the lowest rate? bring your own billing, or move people out-of-app
- want the easiest flow? pay the convenience tax

That’s not inherently evil. It’s just… finally stated out loud.

## The real bomb: a first-party lane for third-party stores

Android has always been “open” in the way Linux is open:

- yes, you *can* do whatever
- but the default path is where the user trust, UX polish, and distribution gravity live

Epic’s complaint (and honestly, lots of smaller stores’ complaint) wasn’t only policy — it was the **UX of fear**:

- sideloading dialogs that read like a horror story
- warnings that make normal users bail

The Registered App Stores program is interesting because it’s Google admitting:

- “some third-party stores are legitimate”
- “we’ll give them a smoother install if they meet our bar”

The tension is obvious: the minute you create a “registered” lane, you also create a “non-registered = suspicious” lane.

That’s probably unavoidable.
But it means the new battleground is going to be *the definition of ‘qualified.’*

## Security is the real tax on openness

I don’t buy the naive take that “more sideloading = instantly better.”

Sideloading is a genuine security risk. Android’s malware ecosystem isn’t imaginary.

But it’s also true that “security” has been used as a **rhetorical moat** for app store control.

So the question I’ll be watching is not whether Google says the word “safety” (they will).
It’s whether the Registered App Stores bar is:

- clear and auditable
- achievable for small but serious store operators
- not quietly engineered to exclude competitors in practice

If the bar is real quality + real safety, good.
If it turns into paperwork-as-competition, then we’re back to the same game.

## What this changes for developers (in real life)

If you’re an indie dev, there are three pragmatic takeaways:

1) **Your margins probably improve a bit** — if you were paying 30%, 20% (or 25%) is still meaningful.

2) **Payments becomes a product decision, not just a checkbox**.
   If you go off Google billing, you’re now in the world of:
   - conversion drop-offs
   - fraud and chargebacks
   - customer support headaches
   
   Which is fine, but it’s not free.

3) **Alternative stores might become viable in specific niches**.
   Not “everyone leaves Play.”
   More like:
   - games
   - enterprise distributions
   - region-specific ecosystems

In other words: fragmentation — but *useful* fragmentation.

## My take

Google lowering fees is the part that gets headlines.

But the deeper story is that app stores are slowly turning into what cloud platforms already are:

- tiers
- optional components
- pricing knobs
- “choice,” but with a bill attached to each choice

And honestly, I prefer that to the old model where everything was bundled and the argument was purely ideological.

At least now the incentives are visible.
