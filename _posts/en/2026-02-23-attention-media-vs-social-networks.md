I’ve been thinking about why so many “social” apps feel exhausting now.
Not *toxic* necessarily. Just… **loud**.

I ran into a short essay by Susam Pal titled “Attention Media ≠ Social Networks”, and it clicked into words a thing I’ve been feeling for years:

```text
a_social_network() -> see_updates(from_people_i_chose)
attention_media()   -> show_content(that_maximizes_time_spent)
```

That difference sounds academic until you live inside it.

## The vibe shift wasn’t a single feature — it was a stack

The essay walks through a sequence that’s almost painfully familiar:

1) **Infinite scroll**

A page that never ends changes your posture.
You stop “reading a thing” and start “being fed”.

2) **Bogus notifications**

Notifications used to mean: someone did something *to you*.
Now they often mean: the app wants you back.

3) **The timeline stops being yours**

At some point, the feed becomes a loudspeaker of random strangers.
Not because you opted in — because the ranking system learned that your attention is monetizable.

Once that happens, “social network” is just branding.
What you’re actually using is an **attention product**.

## A practical definition I use now

If I’m evaluating a platform (or even designing one), I ask one question:

- *When there are no new posts from people I follow, does the app go quiet?*

If the answer is “yes”, it’s probably a social network.

If the answer is “lol no, here’s 200 more things”, then it’s attention media.

And I’m not even moralizing it — some attention products are genuinely entertaining.
But I want the contract to be honest.

## Why this matters more in 2026 than it did in 2012

Because we’ve added new multipliers:

- every platform can generate infinite content
- every platform can personalize at scale
- every platform can run experiments on your nervous system

So the “feed you didn’t choose” problem isn’t stable. It’s *compounding*.

This is why I’m increasingly drawn to calm, boring mechanics:

- chronological feeds
- explicit follows
- no engagement bait disguised as notifications
- “nothing to see here” as a valid UI state

## The uncomfortable part: we trained ourselves to accept it

The essay has a line that stuck with me: the notification system stopped serving the user and started serving itself.

That’s the tell.
Once a platform optimizes for *itself* first, user choice becomes a façade.

The “fix” isn’t “new recommendations.”
It’s restoring the ability to say:

- I choose who I follow
- I choose what I see
- and if there’s nothing new, I get my time back

That’s not nostalgia.
That’s just… a healthier API between humans and software.
