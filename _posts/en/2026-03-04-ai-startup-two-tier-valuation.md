There’s a VC tactic floating around AI deals that sounds like a fun hack until you realize what it really is:
**the same round, two prices, one press release.**

TechCrunch described it as funds splitting a check across two valuation tiers — the lead investor gets a chunk at a lower price, while later money joins at a much higher “headline” valuation so the company can call itself a unicorn.

I get why this exists. I also think it’s quietly toxic if you don’t understand what it does to the next 18 months of your company.

## The five angles I care about

### 1) A “headline valuation” is becoming a product, not a measurement
Once you see valuation as a recruiting tool, a sales signal, and a way to scare off competitors, you stop treating it like “price discovery.”

That’s not inherently evil — startups market themselves — but it means you should read press headlines like marketing copy, not like a cap table.

### 2) The lead investor is basically buying an option (and everyone else is buying the narrative)
If a big chunk of the round clears at $450M and a smaller chunk clears at $1B, the lead is doing something very normal:
- lowering their cost basis,
- paying for control / signaling,
- and using the $1B price as the story everyone repeats.

Meanwhile, other investors are paying a premium to get access.
That premium doesn’t magically increase the company’s fundamentals.
It just increases expectations.

### 3) This shifts the pain to the *next* round (and to employees)
A two-tier round is basically borrowing confidence from the future.
You’re telling the market: “Next time, we must clear *above* the headline number.”

If the blended valuation is lower but the press number is higher, you’ve created a trap:
- raise below the headline → it looks like a down round,
- raise above the headline → you’d better have growth that can carry that price.

And when that trap snaps, employees eat it.
Not because anyone is malicious, but because option value and morale are both extremely sensitive to “are we winning?” narratives.

### 4) Operationally, it encourages speed over durability (the exact failure mode AI startups already have)
If your story requires you to outrun the valuation you just printed, you’re incentivized to:
- hire too fast,
- ship too messy,
- overfit to demos,
- and treat reliability like a future problem.

AI already tempts teams into “it works on stage” engineering.
Two-tier pricing turns that temptation into a deadline.

### 5) If you’re joining an AI startup (or buying from one), ask one annoying question
When someone tells you “we’re a unicorn now,” your job isn’t to be impressed.
Your job is to ask:

- **Was the round priced at one valuation, or multiple tiers?**

You don’t need to be a finance nerd.
You’re just trying to understand whether you’re looking at:
- a clean price signal, or
- a structured deal designed to produce a specific headline.

If you’re an engineer considering an offer, this isn’t about being cynical.
It’s about calibrating risk.
A company under “must beat the headline” pressure behaves differently — in roadmap honesty, in burn discipline, and in how quickly culture turns into politics.

I’m not saying two-tier rounds are always bad.
I’m saying they’re **not free**.
They’re a way to move uncertainty around the system until someone else holds it.
