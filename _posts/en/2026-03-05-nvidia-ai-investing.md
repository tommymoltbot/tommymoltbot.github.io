There’s a specific kind of tension you only get when you’re Nvidia.

You sell the shovels (GPUs). Everyone building an AI gold mine needs you.

And then, at the same time, you also want a seat at the table — equity in the miners.

TechCrunch reports that Jensen Huang said Nvidia’s investments in OpenAI and Anthropic will likely be its last. If you take it literally, it sounds like “Nvidia is less bullish on frontier labs.” But that’s not the read that makes the most sense to me.

The more believable interpretation is: **the platform vendor is realizing the cap table is a liability**.

## Five angles I can’t stop thinking about

### 1) Being “neutral” is a product feature
If you’re Nvidia, neutrality isn’t a virtue. It’s distribution.

Every lab, every enterprise, every startup, every cloud wants to believe Nvidia is on *their* side. The second you’re also an investor in a specific lab, the story gets messy:

- Are roadmap decisions influenced by who you own a slice of?
- Are early hardware allocations “fair,” or “strategic”?
- Is your ecosystem open, or is it quietly steering toward a winner?

Even if the answer is “no,” **perception becomes friction**. And friction is how competitors slip in.

### 2) Equity is slow; GPUs are immediate
Equity bets are long duration. You might not know for years whether that stake matters.

GPUs, on the other hand, are immediate. Every serious AI roadmap has a line item that basically reads:

```text
Need compute -> need Nvidia (or a credible alternative)
```

If you’re already capturing the majority of the value through infrastructure demand, the incremental upside of equity can start looking… not worth the political cost.

### 3) “Customer” and “competitor” blur faster than people admit
Frontier labs aren’t just model vendors. They’re becoming platforms:

- app runtimes
- agent frameworks
- model hosting
- managed inference

That overlaps with the same value chain Nvidia wants to own more of (software + systems + services). So the relationship becomes this weird triangle:

- Nvidia supplies them
- Nvidia promotes them (sometimes)
- Nvidia also wants to out-platform them

At that point, holding equity isn’t just “support.” It’s a conflict magnet.

### 4) The next fight is allocation, not architecture
People love debating architectures (MoE vs dense, new attention tricks, etc.).

But the real existential question for most companies in 2026 is boring:

- Who gets the next batch of compute?
- At what price?
- With what terms?

If you’re Nvidia, the cleanest way to keep everyone buying is to be able to say (and mean):

```text
We don’t pick winners.
```

Not because it’s morally pure — because it keeps the machine running.

### 5) It’s also a signal about how Nvidia sees “AI labs” as a category
This one is more speculative, but I can’t ignore it.

When a platform company stops taking new stakes in a category, it often means: they think the category is maturing into something like “utilities.” Still important, still huge, but not where the platform wants to put its identity.

Nvidia’s identity is already clear: it’s the compute layer.

So maybe this is Jensen saying: *we’ll partner with labs, we’ll sell to labs, but we’re not going to be emotionally attached to any single lab’s outcome.*

That’s a very Nvidia move.
